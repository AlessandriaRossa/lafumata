Last Tuesday evening’s online meeting opened with comments on some [news](https://www.rainews.it/maratona/2026/05/hantavirus-4-in-quarantena-in-italia-in-volo-con-una-delle-vittime-hondius-sbarco-alle-canarie-il-virus-7dc4509e-29a4-4b58-b4e2-398f1b20efc4.html) regarding cases of hantavirus (in the cruise ship MV Hondius), a viral illness transmitted by feral rodents, such as mice and rats.

The strain in question, known as Andes, is one of the rare [hantaviruses](https://www.ilpost.it/2026/05/12/hantavirus-storia/?homepagePosition=10) that have a documented person-to-person transmission: something that represents a non-negligible risk factor. The Italian Ministry of Health has set up a quarantine period of 6 weeks for those that have entered into contact with those infected, given that the virus seems to have an incubation period of up to 40 days.

Per [David Quammen](https://www.repubblica.it/cronaca/2026/05/11/news/david_quammen_covid_hantavirus-425336736), author of the famous essay *Spillover: Animal Infections and the Next Human Pandemic*, today we are less prepared to face the natural process whereby a pathogen acquires the capacity to infect, replicate, and spread between different species, since governments have not yet come up withthe necessary procedures in case of [a pandemic](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/49/la-pandemia-e-le-sue-cause.htm). The  World Health Organisation (WHO) itself has for years been detailing the possible emergence of a currently unknown hypothetical pathogenic agent, known for now as disease X.

The counterposed economic interests, first among them being those of states, impede any attempt at [global coordination](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/47/prove-di-estinzione.htm). The director-general of WHO, Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus, in a recent press conference held together with Spanish prime minister Sanchez, has [declared](https://www.repubblica.it/esteri/2026/05/12/video/hantavirus_loms_al_momento_non_ci_sono_segnali_di_una_possibile_epidemia_su_larga_scala-425339801): *"At the moment there is no sign that we are seeing the start of a larger outbreak. But, of course, the situation could change, and, given the long incubation period of the virus, it's possible we might see more cases in the coming weeks"*. And again: *"[Countries] have sovereignty, so we can't force them to take our protocols. We can only advise and recommend, but I hope they will follow the advice and the recommendations we're making"*.

In the latest editorial of the geopolitical magazine *Limes* ("Lo scisma d'Occidente"[^1], n. 4/26), light was shed on the difficulty present society experiences in controlling itself, especially from a political and military standpoint. Think of the automated systems employed on the battlefield, which represent an enormous risk to the human species:

> "For the first time in the history of humanity we must consider the probability that what will destroy us are the hyper-sophisticated machines that we are assembling. Artificial Intelligence (AI), which promises infinite benefits and even delivers some, has a dark side, whose terrible contours we are already starting to see: autonomous weapons with enormous destructive potential. China, the United States, Russia, and other powers are developing devices so 'intelligent' that they’re able to identify, select, and strike targets without – or against – any human intervention. Applied to hypersonic missiles equipped with multiple nuclear warheads – a field where the Russians are at an advantage – these ‘end of world’ technologies convey an apocalyptic potential that subverts every strategy. If we prove unable to halt it, we will no longer be the ones to choose the enemy. Our weapons will choose for us."

*Limes*, in averting the existential risk tied to the development of artificial intelligence, places confidence in dialogue between the USA, Russia, and China with the end of regulating it. The fact remains that the lofty appeals from capitalists and experts, along with moratoriums on the development of AI, are a fool’s errand. The timetables are quickly shortening, to the point that in mere months technological advancements are of the same magnitude that in the past has required years to reach. When [Marx](https://www.marxists.org/italiano/marx-engels/1844/2/criticahegel.htm) affirmed that the next revolution will be in the name of humanity, he insisted on the fact that the proletariat, negating itself, negates all classes, bringing the species back in harmony with itself and with the rest of nature.

In the meanwhile, [inflation](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c202pgxx89lo) in the United States has jumped to 3.8% due to the rise in energy costs owed to the war with Iran; global public debt has reached the [record number](https://www.reuters.com/world/china/global-debt-hits-record-near-353-trillion-with-signs-move-away-us-2026-05-06/) of 353 trillion dollars, over three times the world’s GDP. The blockade of the Strait of Hormuz (half-closed for almost two months) has had disastrous effects on the world economy. 

*The Economist*, in the article "[Mapping the Iran war's trade disruption](https://www.economist.com/interactive/graphic-detail/2026/05/11/mapping-the-iran-wars-trade-disruption)", claims that the Hormuz crisis has provoked one of the greatest energy shocks in history, and analyses the consequences, with the abrupt decrease in March for instance, when around 1,500 oil tankers worth of monthly transit through the Strait fell down to 180. The countries hit hardest are the Philippines, [India](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/india-will-need-assess-ability-fuel-retailers-bear-losses-oil-minister-says-2026-05-12/), Pakistan, Thailand, and Indonesia (together, they have almost two billion inhabitants). The price of petrol has skyrocketed - in some Asian countries there have been increases of more than 70%. In the USA a gallon of petrol now costs almost 4.60 dollars, up from only 3 dollars in February. The producers of plastic and the industries connected to it are also in crisis. [Ras Laffan](https://eurofocus.adnkronos.com/imprese/lenergia-di-bruxelles-sotto-le-macerie-di-ras-laffan-tra-lincudine-usa-e-il-martello-russo/), a plant in Qatar that produces a third of the world’s helium, has been closed during the conflict due to Iranian attacks. The interruption of the supply has represented a problem for South Korea and Taiwan, countries which are leaders in chip production, that require the gas for cooling the super-magnets used in the production process.

Since in the horizon there is no foreseeable possible end of this war, a further deterioration in the ease of global procurement is probable. The next step, according to some geopolitical analysts, could be the [Strait of Malacca](https://www.fanpage.it/esteri/non-solo-hormuz-il-ruolo-strategico-dello-stretto-di-malacca-nella-sfida-tra-usa-e-cina-lanalisi-di-ispi/), one of the main chokepoints of global commerce.

From [China](https://it.euronews.com/next/2026/04/24/cina-deepseek-lancia-il-nuovo-modello-di-ia-v4-cosa-sapere-nella-corsa-allia), and elsewhere, comes the news of massive investments in [robotics](https://www.chicago86.org/feed-back/articoli/1454-la-marcia-inarrestabile-dei-robot) and in the employment of [artificial intelligence](https://www.ilsole24ore.com/art/ai-cina-cambia-passo-non-piu-modelli-ma-piattaforme-l-economia-reale-AIuRJgpC) in factories. Robots are utilised by the Ukrainian army to hinder the Russian forces on the battlefield, and vice versa. Automated weapon systems are employed by Israel in the Gaza Strip, just as in Lebanon and in the West Bank. Bruce Schneier, expert in information security and cryptography, has put in dispute the classic definition of robot as an autonomous unit able to perceive, elaborate, and act. We no longer must think, he says, of the arrival of an army of automata, since what we have built is a robot the magnitude of the entire world. We live in a society-robot, we are part of it.

In the article "[Che cosa fa l’intelligenza artificiale in guerra](https://www.guerredirete.it/che-cosa-fa-lintelligenza-artificiale-in-guerra/)"[^2], published on the site *Guerre di Rete*[^3], the modalities with which artificial intelligence is employed in conflicts today are described. The big high-tech American businesses have participated in the development of software such as Gospel and Lavender, utilised by the Israeli army in the invasion of Gaza, and Maven, developed since 2017 by Palantir and utilised by the United States as a decision support system. Maven is *"a command and control platform that also offers 'situational awareness in real time', that is, a representation of what happens on the ground, including the positions of friendly and enemy forces, available assets and active threats, and support to operational planning"*.

Large Language Models (LLMs) transform these informational flows into a language comprehensible to military personnel, facilitating the planning of operations. Artificial intelligence companies, both American and Chinese, are increasingly integrated with their respective state apparatuses, contributing to a progressive hybridisation between the tech sector and the military. The clash between Musk and Trump first, and that between Anthropic and the Pentagon later, reflect the growing power of Big Tech.

The human operator is dominated by their perceived means of domination. Automated warfare does not produce soldiers equipped with intelligent systems, but rather soldiers who become prosthetics of the great automaton, as in the case of the modern factory described by Marx in the *Grundrisse* and in Volume I of *Capital*. War reflects society, just as weapons reflect the state of industry. To carry on an armed conflict like the current one, a global integration of industrial and logistical practices is necessary, from the procurement of raw materials (such as oil and microchips) to the control of *chokepoints*. The boundary between military and civilian is increasingly blurred. Our school has [argued for](https://www.quinterna.org/archivio/carteggi/19570105_bordiga_ceglia.htm) the necessity of preventing war at its onset, precisely because, once this type of conflict develops, all of society becomes involved and to go back is extremely difficult.

The [Second World War](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/51/guerra-di-macchine.htm) too was fought using machine systems, but today such systems are autonomising from humanity. This represents an epochal shift, a phase transition. The system responds automatically to the *inputs* it receives from within, and war is a response to a situation becoming untenable. Some sections of the dominant class express concern with such dynamics, understanding the extreme danger they pose (see precisely the last editorial of *Limes*). But, being deprived of the theoretical instruments to find a way out, they flail in the dark.
