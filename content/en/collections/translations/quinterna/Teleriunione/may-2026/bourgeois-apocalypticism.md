The online meeting started with a report on some news concerning the situation of international finance. In particular, we have dwelt on the performance of American government bonds – which are increasingly less requested by investors – and the economic situation in Japan.

Japan is pursuing a “repatriation” of its own capital in order to cope with its domestic expenditure, which is reaching crisis point. The condition of the Japanese economy has, indeed, become substantially worse and its low or even negative interest rates by now belong to the past. Thanks to that monetary policy, Tōkyō has become the world’s main [creditor](https://www.lamiafinanza.it/2026/05/giappone-usa-nuova-crisi-in-arrivo-da-carry-trade-e-ritorni-di-capitale/), all the while accumulating a public debt amounting to about 250% of its GDP, and a private debt constituting 48% of the grand total. Such a mountain of debts was for a long time considered “sustainable” due to  90% of it being held inside national borders, by local banks and investors—a characteristic that, apparently, made it immune to foreign speculation. However, no financial artifice can undo the laws that regulate the capitalistic mode of production: the law of value inevitably presents the bill.

[Japan’s](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/10/crisi_giapponese.htm) problems have their roots in the economic growth of the ‘80s, when the country was considered the USA’s true *competitor*, way before the rise of China. The Japanese economy had reached an inflection point: the automation and robotisation of productive processes determined the overproduction of commodities and capital, which led to the phenomenon of irrational exuberance and parasitic rent. An increasing portion of Japanese capital, facing difficulties in its valorisation inside the national market, has looked for foreign outlets, transforming Japan into one of the world’s greatest creditors through the purchase of American debt securities and more.

Today, to a now out-of-control public debt, a profound demographic crisis has been added (as in most advanced capitalist countries). The aging population entails an increase in social and medical expenditure, in a spiral without any foreseeable end. In this context the Bank of Japan has decided to [increase](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/07/japan-yen-intervention-boj-rate-gap-currency-pressure.html) interest rates, after years of finance at almost-zero cost. According to some financial analysts, the consequences of this change might [extend](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/36/verso_il_collasso_epocale.htm) way beyond the country’s borders.

In the last few months, events such as the American attack on Venezuela, the war in Iran, and the tensions tied to the blockade of the [Strait of Hormuz](https://www.ilgiornale.it/news/guerra/liran-formalizza-controllo-hormuz-funzionerebbero-i-traffici-2666806.html) have moved public attention towards these scenarios. However, the Japanese problem might soon enough assume a worldwide size. The overproduction of commodities and capital, in Tōkyō as in New York and Běijīng, imposes a search for fictitious forms of valorisation that cannot continue forever. Irrational exuberance tends to translate into the formation of bubbles of various natures, which in turn condition the economic policies of states, that are pushed into intervening to avoid their burst, only to end up becoming evermore subject to [autonomised Capital](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/17/autonomizzazione.htm).

At the foundations of the military events of the last few months lies the enormous question of rent, of the control of the flows of value produced by an international proletariat. Our reference texts to delve into this topic are “[Mai la merce sfamerà l’uomo](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/storici/maimerce.htm)”[^1] (1953) and “[La crisi del capitalismo senile](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/ennepiuuno/capitalsen.htm)”[^2] (1984).

Just as the USA is compelled to appropriate the reserves of value (in the form of oil, but not only) in Venezuela and the Middle East, in the same way Iran tries to impose a toll on the ships that pass through the Strait of Hormuz. The current *warpolitik*, as we have written in the leaflet “[Imperialismo con l’acqua alla gola](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/10/imperialismo_acquagola.htm)”[^3] (1 May 2026), *"doesn’t manifest itself so much through the conquest of territories, as through the acquisition of flows of value from competitors, that, for the USA – rentier country par excellence – is the entire world."*

The 16 May issue of the *Economist* displays on the cover the title “[The jobs apocalypse](https://www.economist.com/leaders/2026/05/14/prepare-for-an-ai-jobs-apocalypse)”. In the main article, the consequences of what is commonly defined as "technological unemployment” are analysed, and governments are warned about the fact that, if action is not taken in time by building a social safety net (for example via a universal basic income), serious stability problems will emerge for states, including scenarios of widespread disorders and even outbursts of revolution. Artificial intelligence systems (AI) are becoming widespread in all sectors of society, causing a profound transformation in the relations of production and, as a consequence, in social relations.

In Donald Trump’s recent visit to Běijīng, beyond customs duties, the Iranian question, and trade agreements, the development of AI and its application to the military was certainly discussed too. Every country seeks to be high-tech in both civilian and war industry, but everyone is aware that the [war of machines](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/51/la-sindrome-di-yamamoto.htm), of sensors and of AI might slip away from any control, becoming a danger for the human species.

On Monday 25 May [Pope Leo XIV](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/57/leone-XIV.htm)’s first encyclical, *Magnifica Humanitas. On safeguarding the human person in the time of artificial intelligence*, will be [published](https://www.vaticannews.va/it/papa/news/2026-05/papa-leone-xiv-prima-enciclica-magnifica-humanitas-ia-25-maggio.html). The Church asserts that AI represents a [paradigm shift](https://www.vatican.va/roman_curia/congregations/cfaith/cti_documents/rc_cti_doc_20260304_quo-vadis-humanits_it.html#_ftnref37) in the relation between man and machine, and recommends governments to intervene before it is too late: AI systems utilise data in our stead, wage wars, move commodities and capital. If the encyclical *Rerum Novarum* (pillar of the Church’s [Social Doctrine](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/49/la-dottrina-sociale-della-chiesa.htm)), written by Leo XIII, had the objective of hindering the presence of Marxism among the ranks of the working class through proposing a christian way to “social justice”, *Magnifica Humanitas* acknowledges that we’re facing a new epochal transition.

As we’ve already stated, the bourgeoisie cannot exist without continuously revolutionising the means of production: more robots mean less human labour. The chip is a part of a system, it works inside a machine that is itself part of a machine system, linked to other systems via logistics. Everything, in perspective, is held together by an ensemble of automatic processes, where human intervention is ever more reduced.

In the thread of time “[Precisazioni su ‘Marxismo e miseria’ e ‘Lotta di classe e offensive padronali’](https://www.quinterna.org/archivio/filitempo/028_1949_precisazionioffensive.htm)”[^4] (1949) the validity of Marxist theory is asserted, describing the historical parabola of capitalism:

> *"In an ascending, expanding phase of prosperity: - the number of wage labourers employed in industry increases; - the wage rate increases as well; - the productivity of labour increases as well. In the descending phase of contraction, of alternating crises: -  the total capital-wages ratio increases, but too slowly, or stops; - the increase of the number of proletarians follows suit; - the number of employed workers decreases; - the relative surplus worker population, or reserve army, is formed and enlarges."*

The antagonism between classes, the one between the 99% and the system of the 1%, is a product of capitalism’s working mechanisms. Greater accumulation means a greater number of unemployed and underemployed workers, greater precariousness and misery. Not coincidentally, following the *subprime* mortgage crisis, right in the USA was born a movement such as [Occupy Wall Street](https://www.quinterna.org/pubblicazioni/rivista/30/occupy_the_world_together.htm), that didn’t lay claim to something in particular, but to the demolition of capitalism (“The only solution is World Revolution”—the conclusive statement of the OWS homepage).

During the phase of capitalistic expansion it was easier to deceive proletarians with the fairy tale of progress, from which everyone would’ve benefitted. During the phase of structural crisis the chickens come home to roost: millions of reserveless workers will never enter the job market and the very ideological superstructures of Capital go into crisis. We pay a lot of attention to the “ideological capitulations of the bourgeoisie to Marxism”, because they show that all of society is forced to descend to the level of revolution and deal with it. But only those who are in possession of a theory of social transitions own tools useful to orient oneself in the ongoing social chaos.
